About

The History of Photo Manipulation aims to explore the evolution of photo manipulation from its earliest days to the present, examining the ways in which photographic images have been altered, distorted, and transformed over time. Although we've encountered many examples of artistic and otherwise harmless examples of photo manipulation, the purpose of this timeline is to document purposely misleading photographs, as a way to explore contemporary concerns about dis and misinformation.

Ultimately, the History of Photo Manipulation seeks to shed light on the complex and fascinating history of altered photographic, while also exploring the important ethical and social questions that arise in a world where images can be easily altered and disseminated with just a few clicks of a button.

How did we get here?

Image manipulation, from darkroom to AI

We've heard it before. Truth is dead! You can't trust images anymore! But were we ever able to trust them? How did we get from film cameras to computers making photographs without cameras?

Photo manipulation is not a new phenomenon. It has been around for almost as long as photography itself. From the earliest days of portrait painting and sculpting, people have sought to enhance or modify representations. Darkroom techniques of photo manipulation were used since the mid-1800s: dodging, burning, overexposing, painting over film in the darkroom. A hundred years later, digital photo-editing tools made it easier to edit images, enhance one's own appearance, present false or exaggerated images of political enemies, or edit historical records and perpetuate certain narratives. The rise of AI has compounded this obsession with editing images, fostering deepfakes, beauty filters, and other contemporary trends. AI and contemporary photo manipulation rest on a centuries old-legacy of deception, and more importantly, a human fascination with fiction, with building new worlds and illustrating them, with discovering how far we can push a lie.

Fiction presented as fact is a powerful storytelling device. Images help tell these stories, and photography, in particular, has a unique aura of reliability and truth. Photography's ability to "capture reality" has imbued photos and videos with a promise of authenticity that is hard to undo. Can we undo it? Should we?

When the first photograph was taken, no other medium had come remotely close to a sense of conexion between the real world and its representation. Radio didn't exist, the telephone didn't exist. People couldn't see or hear each other without beying in the same room. And then there was photography. Photography made capturing the world possible, and it gave professional deceivers a tool to tell stories. The allure of deception and the desire to wield its power seeped into our technological innovations. So much, so that the enchanting metaphors used to describe these tools — AI now, photography in the 1800s, and Photoshop in the 80s — often involve the word “magic.” This is the history of that magic; the history of human creativity through photographs, the technologies that led us here, and the precedents that will lead us to the future. This is the history of images as misrepresentation.

Everything started in the darkroom.

Dating back to the 1500s, and maybe earlier, the "camera obscura" was the ancestor of all photographic and cinematic technology. A camera obscura is a dark room with a single, small opening in a wall to admit light. The opening acts as a projection tool, creating an inverted image of the outside world on the opposite wall. The main technical gap between the camera obscura and physical photographs was fixing this projected image onto a surface. It took centuries, but by the late 1700s and early 1800s, many were experimenting with chemical techniques that would make capturing the image possible.
